# Cross-Industry Bottleneck Framework

Use this reference when generalizing Serenity-style AI infrastructure research to other industries. The goal is to find a constrained layer and an underpriced beneficiary, not to copy AI/semi terminology.

## General Formula

> Demand shock -> constrained layer -> scarce capability -> underpriced beneficiary -> falsifiable event -> forward-return audit.

The method works best in industries where physical capacity, regulation, qualification, supply chain, technical know-how, or customer approval creates a delay between demand recognition and profit recognition.

## Industry-Agnostic Workflow

1. Define the demand shock.
   - What changed: regulation, technology, capex cycle, consumer behavior, war/geopolitics, weather, subsidy, platform shift, or replacement cycle?
   - Is the demand shock measurable through orders, usage, capacity additions, permits, budgets, backlog, or pricing?

2. Map the value chain.
   - Break the industry into customers, integrators, component suppliers, equipment vendors, materials, logistics, service providers, and financing.
   - Mark visible consensus winners and less visible enablers.

3. Identify the constraint.
   - Look for long lead times, scarce inputs, permitting, qualification, high switching costs, certification, specialized labor, yield issues, or installed-base lock-in.
   - A true bottleneck should have pricing power, capacity tension, or delayed supply response.

4. Find the beneficiary.
   - Prefer companies with direct exposure to the scarce capability and operating leverage to the bottleneck.
   - Avoid companies mentioned only because they are customers, peers, or obvious mega-cap proxies.

5. Build the evidence stack.
   - Strong: customer signal + supply constraint + orders/backlog/pricing + independent field check.
   - Medium: two confirming sources from different parts of the chain.
   - Weak: management optimism, theme language, single rumor, or chart-only reasoning.

6. Convert to a falsifiable event.
   - Record source URL, event timestamp, ticker, claim type, target role, evidence type, expected mechanism, time horizon, invalidation condition, and benchmark.

7. Audit price path.
   - Check prior runup, event-day liquidity, spread, forward returns, drawdown, runup, and benchmark-relative returns.
   - Classify the event as early discovery, continuation confirmation, late validation, after-fact commentary, or context-only.
   - Choose a benchmark that matches the industry under review. SOXX is only a default for semiconductor and AI-infrastructure claims.

## Transfer Examples

| Sector | Demand Shock | Likely Bottleneck | Possible Beneficiary Type |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Power grid | Data-center load growth | transformers, turbines, switchgear, interconnection queues | equipment suppliers, grid contractors, specialty components |
| Healthcare | new procedure/drug adoption | manufacturing capacity, reimbursement, diagnostics, cold chain | CDMOs, test providers, specialty distributors |
| Defense | rearmament cycle | propulsion, energetics, sensors, shipyard capacity, qualified suppliers | sub-tier suppliers, materials, test equipment |
| Industrials | reshoring/factory automation | robotics integrators, precision parts, controls, labor shortages | automation suppliers, machine vision, niche components |
| Energy | LNG or nuclear buildout | turbines, heat exchangers, uranium conversion/enrichment, permits | specialized equipment, fuel-cycle companies, engineering firms |
| Consumer hardware | platform transition | display, battery, camera module, thermal, assembly yield | component makers, test vendors, materials |

## Discipline

The generalized method should produce research hypotheses, not recommendations. A good output makes the thesis auditable and rejectable. If the bottleneck cannot be linked to a company, timestamp, evidence stack, and benchmark-relative test, keep it as a watchlist idea rather than a learned rule.
